For months I typed into a terminal without noticing that the characters to the left of my cursor were trying to tell me something. Then someone pointed at four tokens and the whole line clicked.

This is Day 2 of a 171-lesson path I am taking publicly, from zero to a Principal Artificial Intelligence Architect role. Week one is the command line. Today is ten minutes of pure observation. No commands that change anything, no installs, no sign-ups. Just learning to read a sign that you already have in front of you.

The analogy I use is a train station. A sign on a station platform does three quiet jobs. It tells you where you are (platform number), who is there with you (which line), and that a train is about to depart (the destination). The prompt on your terminal does the same three jobs, plus one more. Once you can point at each piece and name what it is, the Command-Line Interface (CLI) stops looking like a riddle and starts looking like a sign.

![Side-by-side walkthrough of the shell prompt, Bash on the left with four callouts and PowerShell on the right with three](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/AriesEmber/Principal-AI-Architect-Curriculum/main/modules/M01-first-contact-with-the-terminal/assets/L-002-walkthrough.gif)

**The four questions every prompt answers**

Regardless of which Operating System (OS) you are on, your prompt is trying to answer four questions:

1. Who am I on this machine? (Username.)
2. Which machine am I on? (Hostname.)
3. Where in the filesystem am I standing? (Current directory.)
4. Is the machine waiting for my next command? (Prompt character.)

Write those four questions down on a sticky note before you read the rest of this post. That is the template you will fill in.

![A generic shell prompt with four callouts naming the username, machine name, current directory, and prompt character](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/AriesEmber/Principal-AI-Architect-Curriculum/main/modules/M01-first-contact-with-the-terminal/assets/L-002-anatomy.png)

**On macOS (Zsh)**

Your prompt reads `learner@MacBook-Pro ~ %`.

Four tokens, spaces separate them. `learner` is your username. `MacBook-Pro` is the name of the Mac. `~` is shorthand for your home folder at `/Users/learner`. `%` is the prompt character that Zsh, the default shell on macOS, uses for a regular user.

![Annotated macOS Zsh prompt](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/AriesEmber/Principal-AI-Architect-Curriculum/main/modules/M01-first-contact-with-the-terminal/assets/L-002-prompt-macos.png)

**On Linux (Bash on Ubuntu, Fedora, or Debian)**

Your prompt reads `learner@laptop:~/projects$`.

Same four tokens, packed tighter. Username before the `@`. Hostname between `@` and `:`. Directory between `:` and `$`. The `$` is the Bash default for a regular user. If you ever see `#` in that last position, you are running as root, which is a risk topic for a later lesson.

![Annotated Linux Bash prompt](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/AriesEmber/Principal-AI-Architect-Curriculum/main/modules/M01-first-contact-with-the-terminal/assets/L-002-prompt-linux.png)

**On Windows 11 (PowerShell in Windows Terminal)**

Your prompt reads `PS C:\Users\learner>`.

PowerShell hides two of the four tokens to keep the line short. `PS` is the shell indicator, it tells you PowerShell is running (Command Prompt would show nothing here). `C:\Users\learner` is the current directory in full Windows style, no tilde shorthand. `>` is the prompt character.

The username and hostname still exist, they are just not printed. Type `whoami` and the terminal prints your username. Type `hostname` and it prints your machine name. Both are one-line commands with no side effects.

![Annotated Windows PowerShell prompt](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/AriesEmber/Principal-AI-Architect-Curriculum/main/modules/M01-first-contact-with-the-terminal/assets/L-002-prompt-windows.png)

**The verification**

Close the note. Look away from the screen. From memory, recite the four pieces of your own prompt out loud. Then look back and confirm. If you can name all four without looking, you have the lesson.

These four names are what you will use every time you search for help, every time a tutorial mentions "your working directory," every time a colleague pastes a command into Slack and you have to read it. Today is where they stop being noise.

**Why the prompt is software, not decoration**

The prompt is generated by your shell every time it returns control to you. A shell is the program that sits between you and the computer, taking your text and turning it into work. The template for the prompt is kept in an environment variable called Prompt String 1 (PS1) on Bash and Zsh, or a function called `prompt` on PowerShell. That means every character in the line was chosen by someone, and any of them can be changed, rearranged, colored, shortened, or hidden.

You will change your own PS1 later in the curriculum, once you have earned the right to break and fix your shell without panic. For today, knowing that the prompt is software, not a fixed feature of the window, is enough. It is the difference between "my terminal says some symbols" and "my terminal is answering four questions and I can point at each answer." That is the whole mental shift that separates a nervous user from someone who reaches for the command line first.

Ten minutes of observation. Four names. The next lesson is running your first actual command, which the prompt has been waiting to do since you opened the window yesterday.
